How the SAVE Plan Pause Impacts Student Loan Borrowers Pursuing Public Service Forgiveness

Student loan borrowers working in public service who are striving for forgiveness may need to navigate some challenges due to two ongoing legal cases affecting repayment.

A federal appeals court recently halted the income-driven repayment plan known as Saving on a Valuable Education (SAVE), leading to millions of borrowers being placed in forbearance and impeding their progress toward loan forgiveness.

Key Points

✿ A federal appeals court has recently suspended the income-driven repayment plan, Saving on a Valuable Education (SAVE), resulting in millions of borrowers being placed in forbearance and hindering their progress toward loan forgiveness.

✿ Borrowers eligible for the Public Service Loan Forgiveness (PSLF) program can "buy back" missed payments incurred during forbearance to continue working toward forgiveness.

✿ Some borrowers may need to consider an alternative income-driven repayment plan if they are unable to make up for missed time during forbearance.

Student loan borrowers in public service aiming for forgiveness may need to navigate complex challenges due to two legal cases affecting repayment.

This month, the Department of Education placed loans for Public Service Loan Forgiveness (PSLF) borrowers enrolled in the latest income-driven repayment (IDR) plan into forbearance. The PSLF program provides full loan forgiveness to borrowers who have worked full-time for a public service organization and made 120 qualifying student loan payments.

Many of these qualifying payments were being made under the Saving on a Valuable Education (SAVE) plan, which was intended to offer more favorable terms by reducing payments and simplifying the path to forgiveness. However, the SAVE plan is currently at the center of two court cases, leading to a legal back-and-forth that has culminated in a judge blocking the plan until a final decision is reached.

One borrower posted on X, "I switched to the SAVE plan to make my payments more affordable, and now I'm stuck in forbearance that won't count toward PSLF, which I need to pay off my loans. How is this fair?"

Borrowers Can "Repurchase" Missed Months

If a SAVE plan borrower enrolled in the PSLF program will reach 120 months of payments while in forbearance, they can "repurchase" those months of PSLF credit.

First, you need to demonstrate that you have been a borrower with qualifying employment for 10 years and have an outstanding balance. After submitting your request, you'll receive a buyback agreement detailing the amount that was not paid during forbearance. You must then pay this amount within 90 days to qualify for forgiveness. Borrowers with $0 monthly payments will not need to make any additional payments.

"It's driven by borrowers. They will need to initiate this process themselves," said Megan Walter, a senior policy analyst at the National Association of Student Financial Aid Administrators. "They'll also need to keep accurate records of the months they missed."

"I don't think it will be an easy process, but it's an option for borrowers who prefer not to switch to another IDR program at this time," she said.



The Department of Education noted that borrowers could also enroll in another income-driven repayment plan, such as Revised Pay As You Earn (REPAYE), which was the predecessor to SAVE. However, this option might be challenging, if not impossible, for some borrowers.

Servicers have temporarily suspended online applications for income-driven repayment plans while the Department of Education ensures they are processed correctly. There is currently no timeline for when these applications will become available again.

There’s also uncertainty about whether the REPAYE plan is still available. When SAVE was introduced in August, borrowers on REPAYE were automatically transitioned to the new plan.

Persis Yu, deputy executive director and managing counsel of the Student Borrower Protection Center, noted that recent updates to servicing platforms might have complicated the calculation of payments under the REPAYE plan.
