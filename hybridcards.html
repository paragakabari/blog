Hybrid cars are vehicles that utilize both an internal combustion engine (typically gasoline-powered) and an electric motor for propulsion. They are designed to achieve better fuel efficiency and reduce emissions compared to traditional gasoline-powered cars. Here are some key aspects of hybrid cars:

Types of Hybrids: Parallel Hybrid: Both the electric motor and the gasoline engine can directly power the vehicle. Examples include Toyota Prius. Series Hybrid: The gasoline engine acts as a generator to charge the batteries, which then power the electric motor. Examples include Chevrolet Volt. Plug-in Hybrid: Similar to regular hybrids but with larger batteries that can be charged via an external electric power source. Examples include the Toyota Prius Prime and Chevrolet Volt.

Fuel Efficiency: Hybrids typically achieve better fuel efficiency than conventional vehicles because they can use electric power during low-speed or stop-and-go driving, where internal combustion engines are less efficient.

9. Chevrolet Volt 2024 Hybrid

While discontinued, it was one of the earlier plug-in hybrids, offering electric-only range with a gasoline backup.

The Volt can travel up to 80 kilometers solely on electric power stored in its 16 kWh lithium-ion battery, emitting zero CO2 during this phase. Once the battery's charge is depleted, a gasoline-powered engine generates electricity to drive the electric motor and simultaneously recharge the battery.

The Volt functions primarily as a battery electric vehicle until its battery charge drops below a set threshold from full capacity. At that point, its internal combustion engine activates to power an electric generator, extending the vehicle's driving range as required. At higher speeds, the engine may also mechanically connect (via a clutch) to a generator set, enhancing efficiency by 10% to 15%.
