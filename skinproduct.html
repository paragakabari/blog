"On July 17th, 2024, I felt as though something inside me had shattered completely."

As May approached, I felt like a crumpled figure at the foot of an airport escalator, surrounded by this year's baggage scattered around me. The weight of everything I'd been juggling had finally lifted. There was no need to push forward anymore. My body and mind simply gave in.

I found myself grappling with a lack of motivation and drive, unwilling to engage in even basic tasks like getting out of bed, preparing meals, or socializing. My sense of direction and purpose at work had faded, leaving me feeling tired and burdened. I managed to make it into the office a few times, only to quickly retreat home again.

The first half of this year was a whirlwind of emotions. It included a failed business partnership, rebuilding my company from the ground up, completing my Amen Clinics Brain Health Certification, developing a 12-week online program, encountering work opportunities that fell through, celebrating my daughter's wedding, adjusting to an empty nest, hosting my parents' final visit to the US, supporting a dear friend through a serious health crisis, and learning that two close friends had been diagnosed with cancer.



Juggling All the Things

I worked tirelessly, throughout days, nights, and weekends. Chocolate often substituted for meals. Despite everything, I ensured I was fully engaged for the important moments: dress fittings, the bridal shower, rehearsal dinner, and wedding; walks with my parents; and days spent supporting loved ones in hospital beds. The journey brought both exhilarating highs and profound lows.

And I burnt myself out.

The most frustrating thing is that I know better. I know how to take care of myself. I teach people to take care of themselves for a living.

And I know for a fact I’m not alone.

In our fast-paced world today, many women navigate multiple roles and responsibilities, often resulting in burnout and heightened stress levels.

When National Workaholics Day (July 5) appeared on my calendar, it felt like the perfect moment to address this issue. I'm pleased to report that two months later, I'm experiencing significantly better days—out of ten, nine are now positive. I've found reasons to look forward to each day, laugh and sing more often, reconnect socially, prioritize exercise, and maintain a balanced work life. Taking time for Sabbath has also been crucial. Life feels fulfilling again.

How did I achieve this turnaround? By adopting a holistic approach that includes self-care practices, setting clear boundaries, and embracing a healthier lifestyle. These are the very strategies I advocate for women to prevent and recover from executive burnout.

The Impact of Stress on Your Body

When your body is under profound levels of chronic stress, it undergoes a series of physical changes that can have a significant impact on your overall health. One of the key responses is the activation of the body’s stress response system which releases stress hormones like cortisol and adrenaline. These hormones increase your heart rate, blood pressure, and blood sugar levels, preparing the body for a “fight or flight” response.

When your body experiences prolonged and intense stress, it undergoes physical changes that profoundly affect your overall health. One of the primary responses is the activation of the body’s stress response system, releasing hormones such as cortisol and adrenaline. These hormones elevate heart rate, blood pressure, and blood sugar levels, preparing the body for a "fight or flight" reaction.

When stress becomes chronic, as seen in executive burnout, the consistently high levels of stress hormones can have several adverse effects. Prolonged stress can impair the immune system, making you more vulnerable to illnesses and infections. It can also interfere with your sleep, leading to fatigue and insomnia. Digestive problems, including stomachaches, indigestion, and irritable bowel syndrome, are common with chronic stress. Over time, it can also heighten the risk of serious heart conditions such as high blood pressure, strokes, and heart attacks.

Combine chronic stress with low levels of feel-good neurotransmitters caused by fluctuating hormones, reduced thyroid function, elevated blood sugars, and environmental toxins, and you create a perfect storm for serious health problems.

Finding Balance and Prioritizing Self-Care

At the start of May, we packed up the car and headed to the mountains, taking a much-needed break from everything for four days. I told my dear husband that all I wanted was to sleep until I felt completely rested. We settled into a chair on the deck, letting the 50-mile-an-hour winds sweep away our sorrow, grief, and exhaustion. We enjoyed wholesome food, and the soothing sounds of the flowing water and singing birds replenished our spirits. We walked for miles each day and talked until we had no more words. I found a deep quiet with the Lord, spending hours reading my Bible and journaling. We took time to reflect on our past, assess our present, and consider our future.
