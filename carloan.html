How to Escape an Upside-Down Car Loan

  • by PBM Finance Group,
  • August 9th, 2024
  • in How to Escape an Upside-Down Car Loan

If you find yourself with an upside-down car loan, you're not alone. Many drivers owe more on their vehicles than they’re worth, and this issue is becoming increasingly common. However, no matter how deep you're underwater with your car loan, there are ways to turn things around. Let's explore some strategies to help you escape an upside-down car loan once and for all.

How to Escape an Upside-Down Car Loan

Being upside down on your car loan can be both frustrating and alarming. As the car depreciates in value, you continue to lose money, and there's also the risk of missing payments and facing repossession. But don't worry—there are steps you can take to alleviate the situation. Here’s how you can get back on track:

1. Determine your negative equity.

First, assess the extent of your negative equity by calculating how much you owe compared to your car’s value. To do this, subtract the estimated value of your car—based on a private sale from sources like Kelley Blue Book—from the remaining balance on your loan. This will give you a clear picture of your financial situation.

Remaining Loan Balance – Private Sale Value of Your Car = Negative Equity
2. Choose between selling the car or paying off the loan early.

Once you have a clear understanding of how much you owe beyond the value of your car, you have a few options to consider. Let's explore each one so you can determine the best approach for your situation:



Option 1: Sell the car and take out a personal loan to cover the difference.

If you want to quickly resolve an upside-down car loan, you can sell the car and take out an unsecured personal loan to cover the difference and pay off the remaining car loan.

Although taking on additional debt with a personal loan isn't typically recommended, it can help you avoid a worsening financial situation and prevent the car from dragging you further into debt. Plus, personal loans often come with lower interest rates.

You can maximize your car's sale value by opting for a private sale instead of trading it in at a dealership. List your car on platforms like Craigslist or Cars.com, and inform potential buyers that you need to pay off the car loan before transferring the title.

To streamline the process, you can arrange to meet the buyer at the lender’s office. Use the sale proceeds along with the personal loan to pay off the car loan. Once the bank provides the title, you can immediately hand it over to the new owner.

Additionally, you’ll need to find an affordable temporary car while you save up for a better replacement in the future.



Option 2: Save up the difference and then sell the car.

If your credit score isn't high enough to secure a personal loan, you can delay selling the car until you've saved enough to cover the difference. In the meantime, continue making your car payments as you work towards saving the necessary funds.

There are several effective ways to save the cash needed to cover the difference—such as creating a budget, reducing unnecessary expenses, or taking on a side job. Stay focused and persistent, as being upside down on your loan means you’re losing money. Your goal should be to eliminate this loan as soon as possible!



Option 3: Keep the car and pay off the loan early.

If you want to escape an upside-down car loan but prefer not to sell your car, you could consider paying off the loan early. However, before making that decision, ask yourself:

1. Can you pay off the car within the next two years?

2. Is the total value of all your vehicles less than half your annual income?

If you answer "no" to either question, selling the car might be a better option. If you answer "yes" to both, prioritize paying off the loan early. The sooner you do so, the more money you'll save and the quicker you'll be free from the underwater car loan!

These are the three main strategies for addressing an upside-down car loan. The best approach depends on your individual situation, but your ultimate goal should be to resolve either the car or the loan as quickly as possible.



3. Settle any remaining debt.

You’re not truly free from an underwater car situation until you've addressed all the related debt. Whether you're focusing on paying off the car loan directly or tackling a smaller personal loan, it's crucial to get serious about eliminating it.

The key is to reduce your expenses and boost your income. Identify areas where you can cut spending and find ways to increase your monthly earnings. It may require some hard work and making sacrifices, but these efforts are temporary. Once you've cleared the debt, you'll be free from the burden of an upside-down car loan and the financial strain it causes.



What to Avoid When Dealing with an Upside-Down Car Loan

Trade in the Car

Trading in your upside-down car for another vehicle might initially seem like a good solution. However, the reality is that trading in a car with negative equity doesn’t eliminate the debt—it simply transfers it to your new car loan, leaving you with even more debt.

Rolling over your negative equity into a new loan only deepens your financial hole. To avoid perpetuating the debt cycle, focus on paying off the current loan in full and save up to buy your next car with cash.

Refinance the Loan

Some may suggest refinancing your underwater car loan as a solution, but this approach doesn’t truly resolve your issue. Even if you secure a lower interest rate, you’ll still owe more on the car than it’s worth. While refinancing may slow the rate at which you’re sinking, it doesn’t stop the problem.

Refinancing might give the impression that you’re addressing the situation, which could lead you to relax your efforts. Instead, focus on creating a solid plan to eliminate your loan and diligently work towards achieving that goal.

Surrender Your Car

You might think that simply returning the car to your lender will resolve your upside-down loan, but that’s not the case.

In reality, you should never voluntarily surrender your car. Your lender will sell it at auction for a fraction of its value and then pursue you for the remaining balance, which will likely exceed the original amount you owed.

While it’s understandable to feel desperate to get rid of the car, the best approach is to sell it yourself. This way, you’ll receive a higher sale price and avoid the complications of a lawsuit. Paying off a personal loan or saving up to cover the difference is typically more manageable and less stressful than dealing with legal action from your lender.

Borrow Money from Family or Friends

If you can't secure a personal loan to cover the loan difference, you might consider asking friends or family for help. However, borrowing from people you know can quickly become uncomfortable.

While a parent or friend could provide the funds needed to resolve your car loan, it often leads to significant stress and awkwardness. The obligation to repay them can strain your relationship, as they may constantly inquire about repayment and scrutinize your spending. It's generally better to avoid putting personal relationships at risk for financial matters.
